I had exactly 48 hours between flights. Most sensible people would have booked a hotel near Narita and called it a night. I, however, am not most sensible people. I bought a Suica card, flagged down a Narita Express, and dove headfirst into Tokyo.

This is what two days in one of the world's greatest cities looks like when you refuse to waste a single minute.

## Day 1: Shibuya → Harajuku → Shinjuku

### Morning: The Crossing

There's a reason every travel photographer eventually ends up at **Shibuya Crossing**. At peak hours, over 3,000 people cross simultaneously from six directions — it's organized chaos elevated to art form. I got there at 8 AM, found a window seat at the Starbucks overlooking the intersection, and watched the city wake up over a matcha latte.

From Shibuya I walked north through **Harajuku** — past the wild street fashion of Takeshita Street and into the calm of **Meiji Shrine**. The contrast is remarkable: five minutes from neon-soaked streetwear boutiques, you're walking a gravel path through ancient cedar trees.

> _Tip: Visit Meiji Shrine early. By 10 AM tour buses start arriving and the atmosphere changes completely._

### Afternoon: Ramen School

Lunch was at a standing ramen bar in a basement somewhere near Shinjuku station — I found it by following my nose. No English menu. I pointed at what the person next to me was eating (a rich tonkotsu with chashu pork) and was rewarded with the best bowl of ramen of my life.

The broth. The noodles. The slow-cooked egg perfectly split to reveal a jammy centre. I understood, in that moment, why people fly to Japan specifically for the food.

### Evening: Shinjuku After Dark

**Shinjuku** at night deserves its own novel. I wandered through **Kabukicho**, Japan's largest entertainment district, watched a busker play shamisen in an underpass, and eventually found my way to **Golden Gai** — a network of tiny alleyways packed with bars that seat maybe 8 people each.

I ended up at a bar run by a retired jazz musician who spoke three words of English. We communicated through music, Google Translate, and whisky. 10/10, would do again.

## Day 2: Asakusa → Akihabara → Odaiba

### Morning: Old Tokyo

**Asakusa** is the closest you'll get to historical Edo-period Tokyo. The approach to **Senso-ji Temple** through **Nakamise-dori** — a long shopping street lined with traditional craft stalls — is a perfect sensory overload of incense, colour, and good-natured haggling.

I bought:
- A folding fan with a Mt. Fuji print
- Two packets of ningyo-yaki (small sponge cakes shaped like traditional characters)
- A lacquered chopstick set I did not need but absolutely did not regret

### Afternoon: Akihabara Deep Dive

**Akihabara** is what happens when technology and pop culture have a baby and that baby is allowed to do whatever it wants. Multi-storey electronics shops, anime merchandise towers, maid cafés, retro gaming emporiums. I spent three hours here and barely scratched the surface.

The highlight: a shop that sells exclusively vintage Game Boys. Walls covered floor-to-ceiling in every colour variant ever produced. I resisted buying one. Barely.

### Evening: Teamlab Planets

If you do one "touristy" thing in Tokyo, make it **teamLab Planets** in Toyosu. It's an immersive digital art experience unlike anything else — rooms filled with infinity mirrors, floating flowers, and water you walk through that reacts to your movement.

I went in sceptical. I came out genuinely moved.

## Practical Notes

**Getting around**: The Tokyo Metro is clean, punctual, and runs everywhere. Buy a Suica card at the airport — it works on virtually every train and bus line, and you can also pay at many convenience stores.

**Food budget**: I ate incredibly well for roughly ¥2,000–¥3,500 per meal (about $15–$25). Tokyo's convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) are genuinely great for quick cheap meals.

**Stay**: I crashed at **Millennials Shibuya** — small capsule-style pods, but the common areas are beautiful and the location is unbeatable.

**Would I come back?** Before I even left for the airport, I was planning my return trip. Tokyo has a specific energy I've never found anywhere else — intensely busy, yet somehow never stressful. I'll be back.
